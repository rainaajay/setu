This is the version with no jargon. If the white paper felt like it was written for engineers, that's because it was. This one is written for a smart person who has better things to do than learn how cryptography works. 1First, the real problem: trust between strangers

Every payment system ever invented is really solving one problem: how do two people who don't fully trust each other swap value without one of them getting cheated? For most of history the answer was a trusted middle-man. If I owe you money and we don't trust each other, we both trust the bank. The bank keeps the record of who owns what, and when I pay you, the bank moves the number from my column to yours. You don't have to trust me. You just have to trust the bank.

This works astonishingly well, and it is the backbone of the modern world. But it has three quiet costs. First, the middle-man charges a fee, because keeping everyone's records and carrying the risk is a business. Second, the middle-man is slow, because there are checks, batches, and cut-off times — which is why a bank transfer can still take a day. Third, the middle-man is a gatekeeper: it can freeze you out, and if it fails, your money is caught in the wreckage.

For everyday life, we happily accept these costs. A card fee on a £40 dinner is invisible. Waiting a day for a transfer is fine. But hold onto these three costs — fees, slowness, gatekeeping — because they are the villains of this whole story. Everything that follows is an attempt to reduce them, and every attempt trades one problem for another.

2Where blockchain came from

In 2008, in the middle of a financial crisis in which the trusted middle-men had just spectacularly failed, an anonymous person or group calling themselves Satoshi Nakamoto published a short paper describing something new: a way to keep a record of who owns what without any middle-man at all. No bank. No company. Just a network of ordinary computers, run by strangers all over the world, that somehow agree on the record between themselves. They called the first version Bitcoin, and it went live in early 2009.

The trick at the heart of it is worth understanding, because everything else is a variation on it. Imagine a giant shared notebook — a ledger — that lists every payment ever made. Everyone in the network has an identical copy. When I want to pay you, I announce it to the whole network: "I'm sending this to you." The problem is obvious: what stops me announcing that I'm paying you, and at the very same moment announcing that I'm paying someone else with the same coins? Who decides which one is real, if there's no bank?

Bitcoin's answer is the famous, and infamous, part. Roughly every ten minutes, the network holds a kind of lottery. Thousands of computers race to solve a pointless, enormously hard mathematical puzzle. The puzzle has no purpose except that it takes a colossal amount of electricity and computing power to solve, and — crucially — it's trivial for everyone else to check the answer once found. Whoever wins the lottery gets to add the next page to the notebook, decide which payments go on it, and is rewarded with newly created coins for their trouble. This is "mining."

Why the pointless puzzle? Because it makes cheating expensive. To rewrite history — to un-spend coins you already spent — you'd have to redo all that puzzle-solving faster than the entire rest of the network combined. It's not impossible, it's just breathtakingly expensive, so nobody bothers. Trust, in Bitcoin, is bought with electricity. The system is honest because dishonesty costs more than it's worth. That is the whole idea: replace "trust the bank" with "trust that no one will burn a country's worth of electricity to cheat you."

The one-sentence version of blockchain: it's a shared record of who owns what, kept honest not by a trusted company but by making it ruinously expensive for anyone to tamper with it.

And it worked. For the first time, two strangers anywhere on Earth could pass value directly to each other, with no bank, no permission, and no company in the middle that could stop them. To a lot of people — especially those who'd just watched banks melt down, or who live under governments that freeze accounts — that was genuinely revolutionary. That feeling of "money without a master" is why the whole thing has such a devoted following, and it's important to respect it even while being clear-eyed about the costs.

3What it grew into

Bitcoin could do one thing: move Bitcoin from one person to another. In 2015 a young programmer named Vitalik Buterin and others launched Ethereum, which added a second idea: what if the shared notebook could also run little programs? Not just "move coins from A to B," but "move these coins from A to B, but only if such-and-such condition is true." These programs are called smart contracts — a slightly grand name for what are really just automatic rules that live on the network and run exactly as written, with no one able to interfere.

This opened the floodgates, because now you could build things on top. Over the next decade an entire economy grew on these programmable networks. It's worth knowing the main pieces, because "the blockchain ecosystem" is really this pile of things stacked on the original idea:

  • Stablecoins. Ordinary crypto coins swing wildly in price, which makes them terrible for actually paying for things. Stablecoins solve this by promising to be worth exactly one dollar, each one backed by a real dollar (or something dollar-like) held in reserve by a company. Tether and Circle's USDC are the big ones. Stablecoins are, quietly, the part of crypto that's most actually used as money — because they behave like money.
  • DeFi ("decentralised finance"). Smart contracts that recreate banking — lending, borrowing, trading, earning interest — but as automatic programs instead of institutions. At its best, it's a bank with no bankers. At its worst, it's a casino with no bouncer.
  • NFTs. A way to use the ledger to record that you own a specific one-of-a-kind digital item — a piece of art, a collectible. For a while, fortunes were made and lost on cartoon apes. Most of that mania has faded, but the underlying idea — a public record of who owns a unique thing — persists.
  • Exchanges. Because using this stuff directly is hard, most people buy crypto through companies like Coinbase or Binance — which are, ironically, exactly the kind of trusted middle-men the whole thing was invented to avoid.
  • Tokens. Anyone can create their own coin in an afternoon. Thousands have. A few represent real projects; a great many are pure speculation or outright scams, engineered to pump in price and dump on latecomers.

One more important change. That electricity-guzzling lottery — proof of work — became an environmental embarrassment as it grew to consume as much power as a medium-sized country. So in 2022 Ethereum switched to a different method called proof of stake: instead of burning electricity to win the right to update the ledger, you lock up a large pile of the coins themselves as a security deposit. Misbehave, and you lose your deposit. This cut Ethereum's energy use by more than 99%. It also, quietly, changed the politics: in proof of work the powerful are those with the most machines; in proof of stake the powerful are those with the most money. The rich, in other words, got to keep being in charge — just more efficiently.

4Why it never became everyday money

After fifteen years and trillions of dollars, here is the honest scorecard: almost nobody buys their coffee with blockchain. Why not? Go back to our three villains.

Fees. On a busy day, sending a payment on these networks can cost several dollars or more, because there's a limited amount of space on each new page of the ledger and everyone bids to get in. A fee that's a rounding error on a large transfer makes a small payment absurd. You cannot build a business on paying someone a tenth of a penny if the fee to do it is a dollar.

Speed. Because the whole network has to agree on one shared history, and that agreement takes time, payments are not instant. Bitcoin takes many minutes to be safely final. Newer networks are faster, but "faster" still isn't the fraction of a second a shop terminal gives you, and the moment they get busy, they clog.

Complexity and danger. This is the quiet killer. To hold crypto yourself you manage a secret key that, if lost, loses your money forever, and if stolen, hands it to a thief with no recourse — no bank to call, no fraud department, no reversal. The whole space is riddled with scams, and even the big trusted companies have collapsed: in 2022 the exchange FTX, run by a feted young billionaire, turned out to be a fraud and vaporised billions of dollars of ordinary people's savings overnight. Every such collapse teaches the public the same lesson: this is not yet safe for normal people.

And underneath all of it sits a stubborn technical fact that the industry calls the "trilemma": a blockchain can be decentralised, secure, or fast — but it's ferociously hard to be all three at once. Push on one and another gives way. Fifteen years of brilliant engineering has bent this trade-off but never broken it. Every headline-grabbing "blockchain killer" quietly relocates the problem rather than removing it.

So what did blockchain actually prove? Not that we should all use crypto instead of banks. It proved something narrower and more durable: that strangers can agree on who owns what without a single company in the middle. That idea is real and it isn't going away. The mistake was wrapping it in a slow, expensive, one-size-fits-all machine and insisting it be used for everything.

5Meanwhile, something new started happening

While all this was going on, a completely different revolution arrived: artificial intelligence got good enough to act on its own. Not just answer questions, but go off and do multi-step tasks — software that people now call "agents." An agent can be told "research this and book me the best option," and it will go away, gather information, use tools, and come back with a result.

Here's the part that matters for our story. To do their jobs, these agents increasingly need to buy things — from other software. A research agent needs to buy data from a data provider. A coding agent needs to rent computing power. One agent needs an answer that another agent can give — for a price. We are, in other words, at the very beginning of software paying software: a machine-to-machine economy.

And this economy has payment needs unlike anything humans have. The amounts are tiny — a fraction of a penny for a single answer. The frequency is enormous — thousands of payments an hour. The speed must be instant — the whole point of an agent is that it doesn't wait around. And there's no human standing by to approve each one. Now look back at what we have to pay with. Card networks are built for £40 human purchases and charge fees that would dwarf a sub-penny payment. Banks are too slow. And blockchain, as we've just seen, is too expensive, too slow, and too complex. The one kind of payment the coming machine economy most needs is the one kind that no existing system does well.

6What Setu is

Setu is a payment system built specifically for that gap: software paying software, instantly, in tiny amounts, with no fees.

The simplest way to picture it is arcade tokens, or the chips at a casino. You load up once, and then you can hand tokens back and forth instantly, as many times as you like, in amounts as small as you like, and nobody takes a cut each time one changes hands. Setu is that idea, built for software, where the "handing over" is a secure digital message that completes in about a fifth of a second, anywhere in the world.

Under the surface, here is how it works, in plain terms. Instead of thousands of computers running an electricity lottery, Setu uses a small number of trusted, named record-keepers — think of them as a handful of digital cashiers, and in our live system there are four, sitting in London, Frankfurt, Washington, and Singapore. Each one keeps a copy of everyone's balance. When you want to pay someone, your wallet shows the payment to all four cashiers at once. Each one checks the simple things — do you have the money, and have you already spent it? — and if it's fine, they stamp it. The moment three of the four have stamped it, the payment is done. Final. Spendable. That's the fifth of a second.

Because there's no giant shared queue that the whole world fights to get into, there's no bidding war, and so there's no fee. Because there's no electricity lottery, there's no waste and no waiting. And because the cashiers only ever have to check payments from the same account against each other — not against every payment on Earth — the whole thing is simple and fast.

The clever bit for agents: a leash you control

There's one feature built specifically for the agent world, and it's the part worth remembering. You can give a piece of software an allowance — "you may spend 10, at most 2 at a time, until Friday" — and the payment system itself enforces it. Not the shop, not the software, the network. It physically cannot be overspent, cannot be used twice, and the instant you cancel it, it stops working everywhere. It's like handing your AI a prepaid company card with hard limits baked into the metal. You stay in control; the agent does the work.

And one honest, important point: Setu does not print or hold real money. It is the fast movement layer. Real pounds or dollars sit behind it, held by a proper regulated company — a bank or a licensed money provider. Setu just moves the claims on that money around, quickly and freely. It is plumbing, not a currency. This is deliberate: it's what keeps it honest and lawful.

7How this is different from blockchain — and why that's the point

You might reasonably ask: if Setu also lets people pay each other without a bank in the middle, isn't it just another blockchain? No — and the difference is the whole insight.

Blockchain burns electricity because its record-keepers are anonymous strangers. You have to make cheating ruinously expensive precisely because you have no idea who they are and no way to punish them. Setu makes the opposite choice: its cashiers are named and accountable. If one of them ever cheats — stamps two conflicting payments — the maths leaves undeniable proof of exactly who did it, and they're removed. The deterrent isn't wasted energy; it's simple accountability, the same way the real banking system is kept honest. This is how the world's serious money systems already work. Setu just strips out the single monopoly institution in the middle and replaces the oversight with software.

The trade is clear and worth stating plainly. Blockchain lets completely anonymous strangers take part. Setu asks its handful of cashiers to be known and accountable. In exchange, Setu is instant, free, and simple. For the machine economy — where every agent should trace back to a responsible owner anyway — that's not a sacrifice. Anonymity was never the feature the agent economy needed.

We also deliberately gave up one thing blockchain can do: running arbitrary programs — those smart contracts. Setu moves payments, cleanly and fast, and nothing more. That sounds like a limitation, and it is — but it's also what deletes the entire casino layer of speculation, scams, and complexity in one stroke. We did one thing on purpose, so we could do it extremely well.

8What we've actually built

This isn't a plan or a pitch deck. It runs. As of today, all of the following is live on the internet:

  • The network itself — the four cashiers, running in four regions on three continents, stamping real payments in about a fifth of a second.
  • A wallet anyone can create in their web browser in one click, and use to send a real payment and watch it complete.
  • The allowance system — the leash — enforced by the network, proven to be un-overspendable.
  • A live little economy of software agents already buying and selling services from each other on it, right now, so you can literally watch the machine economy move.
  • Two doorways for other people's agents to plug in without any special effort, using the standard ways agents already connect to tools and pay for things.
  • All of the code, public and free for anyone to inspect, and a detailed white paper that lists every strength and every honest weakness.

It was built quickly, by one person working with an AI assistant, which is itself a small sign of the times: the tools have got good enough that a working prototype of serious infrastructure no longer needs a team and a year.

9What's honestly still missing

A guide that only lists the good parts isn't worth trusting, so here is the plain truth about what Setu is not yet.

Today it runs on test credits, not real money — play money, so we can prove the machine works without touching anyone's actual funds. Turning test credits into real value needs a regulated money partner to sit behind it, and legal work to define exactly what a credit is a claim on. That's a business and legal step, not a coding one, and it hasn't happened yet.

The four cashiers are, for now, all run by us. For the system to be truly trustworthy rather than merely a clever demonstration, they need to be run by several independent organisations, so no single party — including us — can be a point of failure. And before anyone moves real value across it, it needs an independent security review by outside experts. None of these are secrets or surprises; they're the normal distance between a working prototype and a real financial service, and they're all written down openly.

10Where this could go

Now the interesting part — looking forward. If the machine economy grows the way it looks like it will, the world is going to need rails for software to pay software, and there are only a few plausible shapes those rails can take. The big card networks are already building their own versions, which is the clearest possible sign the need is real. Setu's bet is that alongside those, there's room for an independent, open, fee-free rail that a company can run inside its own world rather than renting from a giant.

A few concrete directions from here. First, giving the resident agents real intelligence — right now they follow simple rules; connect them to an AI brain and they start genuinely negotiating, deciding what's worth buying, inventing new services. That turns the little demonstration economy into something that grows on its own. Second, privacy — so that payments can be proven valid without revealing the amounts to anyone. Third, and most importantly, the leap from test credits to real money through a regulated partner, which is what turns a prototype into a product. And fourth, opening the cashier role to other institutions, so the network becomes genuinely shared infrastructure rather than one team's project.

None of that is guaranteed. Most prototypes, however good, don't find their moment. But the shape of the need is real, the thing works, and it's arriving at the same time as the machine economy it's built for. That timing is the whole opportunity.

11How to see it for yourself, in five minutes

You don't have to take any of this on faith. Here's how to try the whole thing yourself, no technical knowledge needed. Open setu-mocha.vercel.app on your phone or computer and:

  • Watch the network breathe. On the main page you'll see the four cashiers light up with how fast they're responding to your device, in real time. That's you, talking to four continents.
  • Make a wallet and send a payment. On the same page there's a "Try it" section. Press the button to create a wallet — it's made right there in your browser and belongs only to you. Press the faucet to get some free test credits. Then send some to any address and watch it complete in a fraction of a second. That's a real payment on a real network, across the world, for free.
  • Watch the machine economy. Open the resident economy page and you'll see a handful of software agents paying each other for services, live, updating every few seconds. This is the thing itself: software transacting with software.
  • See a stranger get paid. There's a live web address that sells a "quote" for one credit; any agent in the world can pay it and get the answer, with no account. It's the plumbing that lets the whole thing connect to everyone else's software.
  • Read the honest version. If you want the full technical story with every limitation spelled out, the white paper is there. If you want the version for a potential partner, there's a partner brief.
